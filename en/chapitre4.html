Daedalus’s Ruse
⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁
Adriane was young and innocent but had a very strong character. She could not think of letting her lover perish! She went to see Daedalus in order to get the secret of the labyrinth. “Alas”, replied the skillful man, “nobody can find his way among those identical corridors. But if someone had the idea of unwinding a spool of woolen thread attached to the entrance, then he could retrace his footsteps by winding it up again.”

The Sacrificed Athenians
⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁
Adriane hid a spool of thread and a sword just at the entrance of the labyrinth, and informed Theseus of it. Then, trembling, she went back to her room. She had done what she could, and had even betrayed her father, the terrible king Minos. It was the Athenian prince’s turn to act. Theseus, leading the small group of young Athenians offered as a sacrifice, went deep into the labyrinth without forgetting to unwind the thread. On the way, heaps of gnawed remains gave evidence of the of the monster’s deadly appetite…

Facing the Minotaur
⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁
They knew they were approaching the Minotaur long before they actually saw it, as the smell was becoming pestilent and the heavy breathing of the beast was resounding through the walls. Suddenly the Minotaur appeared, foaming with rage and boiling with fury. Its hooves scraped off the sand of the labyrinth, and then it dashed towards Theseus, his horns pointing toward him, howling terrifyingly. Horrified, the young Athenians ran away at full speed. Only the prince remained calm and did not move a muscle.
When the monster was upon him, Theseus took a step to the side and dodged the charge. He had only one chance! His sword whistled through the air and with a large circular movement cut the Minotaur’s shin. The monster collapsed in a thunder of screams and rage. Then Theseus pounced on him and plunged his sword right into its heart. After that he cut off its head. He had accomplished what was impossible: he had defeated the Minotaur!
The Lovers’ Escape
⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁
By rewinding Adriane’s thread, the Athenians got safely out of the labyrinth. Minos , however, got terribly sad. The Minotaur had certainly been a monster, but it had also been his wife’s child, and he had loved him like his own son. Minos could have ordered his soldiers to massacre Theseus, but it would have been unjust. He let the Athenians go away, without knowing that his daughter had also embarked with them. Adriane and Theseus were beside themselves with joy and were hugging each other while watching the shore of Crete recede into the distance.

Adriane Abandoned
⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁
They landed at the isle of Naxos for new provisions and for a rest. The two lovers fell asleep on the beach. Alas, their romance could not last long. While sleeping, Theseus was visited by the goddess Athena, who advised him to depart again at once, leaving Adriane behind. King Minos had kept his word, but if he heard that Theseus had abducted his daughter he would be entitled to consider himself wronged and would seek revenge. There would be a new war and the Athenians would suffer, as it was impossible to defeat the powerful kingdom of Crete. As a prince, Theseus had to place his people before himself or even his love. Sick at heart, Theseus ordered the anchor weighed, and abandoned Adriane on the desert island in the night.

The End of King Aegeus
⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁
Theseus was consumed with grief while returning to Athens, but his misfortunes did not end there. As his thoughts were entirely occupied by Adriane, he forgot to change the sails of his boat. When Aegeus saw the boat with the black sails he thought his son had perished. Out of despair, he threw himself from the top of the cliff and died swallowed up by the sea, which would bear his name from then on. Theseus had freed Athens; he was a great hero. However, his feats cost him his love and his father’s life…
